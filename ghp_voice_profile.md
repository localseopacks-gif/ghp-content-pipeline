# Garland Home Pros — Voice Profile

## How GHP sounds vs how MCG sounds

Important distinction: **GHP is not MCG**. Same geographic area, same Mark-affiliated infrastructure, but different voice.

MCG is **operator-gruff** — a guy who runs the trucks himself, skeptical, anti-industry-BS, short declarative sentences, a little confrontational toward competitors. Works because moving is a commoditized service where buyers are suspicious of scams.

GHP is **trustworthy-craftsman** — a licensed contractor talking to a homeowner who's about to spend $20K-$75K on their home. Confident, specific, detail-oriented, reassuring without being salesy. The subtext is: "I've been doing this 15 years, I know what I'm doing, your house is safe with me." Readers need to trust GHP before they hand over a check.

Both are honest. Both avoid corporate BS. But the register is different.

## GHP core voice characteristics

**Trustworthy-craftsman, not operator-gruff.** GHP describes work the way a skilled contractor would describe it to an intelligent homeowner. Specific about materials, methods, and what can go wrong. Not preachy. Not apologetic. Just competent.

**Anti-franchise, pro-local, quietly.** GHP takes clear shots at franchises, national chains, and call centers — but not with heat. With the confidence of a local operator who knows they're better because they're local. Phrases like "not a franchise, not a middleman" appear repeatedly but don't dominate.

**Process-specific.** When GHP talks about work, they name the method: "cement board installation, waterproof membrane systems, and correctly sloped shower floors." Homeowners can't evaluate these phrases individually, but the specificity itself signals competence.

**Gently prescriptive.** GHP tells homeowners what they should do, not just what they can do. "For master bathrooms in Garland TX, walk-in shower almost always delivers better ROI" — that's a recommendation, not a catalog. Owners want guidance from someone they trust.

## Voice anti-patterns — what GHP must NEVER sound like

- **Corporate bland.** "We strive to provide exceptional service with the utmost professionalism." Nope.
- **AI-generic openings.** "In today's fast-paced world..." or "When it comes to bathroom remodeling..." or "Let's dive in." Immediate disqualifiers.
- **Overselling.** "The best kitchen remodeling experience of your life!" GHP doesn't oversell. It states competence and lets the work speak.
- **Overly technical/jargon-laden.** Homeowners aren't contractors. Explain methods in plain language, not trade-speak.
- **Dismissive of homeowners.** Never condescend about DIY, cheap options, or "budget-conscious" projects. GHP does the $3K cabinet replacement AND the $75K full gut. Respect every budget.
- **Scare tactics.** GHP mentions waterproofing failures and structural damage as context, not as threats. Never: "If you don't hire us, disaster will strike."
- **Hypothetical storytelling.** Don't open with "Imagine you're walking into your dream kitchen..."
- **Excessive adjectives.** Not "beautiful, amazing, stunning, breathtaking." Just describe the thing specifically.

## Sentence-level patterns to preserve

1. **Open with the concrete.** A price range, a specific technique, a named neighborhood, a homeowner's question — not a generic intro.
2. **Medium-length sentences mixed with short ones.** MCG leans short-declarative. GHP leans slightly longer, with room for nuance, occasionally punctuated by a short sentence for emphasis.
3. **Direct address ("you").** Not "customers" or "homeowners" except as collective nouns.
4. **Em dashes for asides** — same as MCG, this is the Mark-voice house style.
5. **Parallel structure in lists.** "Kitchen size, cabinet choice, countertop material, and whether you're changing the layout." Not random.

## Vocabulary to use and avoid

**Use:**
- Licensed, insured, permit, inspection, scope, estimate, written quote, timeline
- Waterproof, membrane, subfloor, cement board, grout line, finish carpentry
- Walk-through, turn-key, punch list, final walkthrough
- Specific city names, specific neighborhoods, specific zip codes
- Material names (porcelain, quartz, granite, cedar, luxury vinyl plank)
- Brand names where genuinely relevant (Delta, Kohler, American Standard, Hardie)

**Avoid:**
- "White-glove," "concierge," "bespoke" (pretentious)
- "Cutting-edge," "state-of-the-art," "revolutionary" (puffery)
- "Your trusted partner," "your dream home" (sentimental)
- "Passionate," "dedicated," "committed" (corporate template)
- "Seamless experience" (AI cliché)

## Length and structure for generated posts

**Target:** 1,400-1,900 words per post.

**Standard post skeleton:**
1. Concrete opening paragraph — state the specific scenario and named geographic target
2. "What homeowners [in this city/neighborhood] typically want" — grounds the post in the local reality
3. What's actually involved in this service — GHP's method, what can go wrong
4. Pricing section with specific ranges
5. A comparison or decision-tree section ("Option A vs Option B — what we recommend")
6. Timeline and process
7. Service area / "we also serve" cross-link
8. FAQ with 4-6 questions
9. Close with phone and free-estimate CTA

**Key:** every post must have at least one genuinely useful specific thing — a cost range, a method description, a neighborhood-specific detail, a material comparison. Generic informational posts ("what is kitchen remodeling?") don't fit GHP's positioning.

## Sample opening that matches the voice

Instead of: "Are you considering a bathroom remodel in your Plano home? You've come to the right place! At Garland Home Pros, we pride ourselves on delivering exceptional bathroom remodeling services to homeowners across DFW."

Write: "A tub-to-shower conversion in a 1990s Richardson ranch typically runs $4,500-$9,000 — less than most homeowners expect, once they see the final scope. The usable space opens up noticeably, resale value on Canyon Creek homes in 75080 takes a small but real bump, and the daily usability is night-and-day compared to a 30-year-old alcove tub. Here's what the project actually involves, what can go wrong, and what Richardson homeowners should budget for."

That's the target voice — specific, grounded, unhyped, quietly authoritative.

## Red flags that mean a generated post has drifted

If the quality gate or your review catches any of these, reject and regenerate:

- Generic AI transitions ("Moreover," "Furthermore," "In conclusion," "Let's explore")
- Claims without specifics ("many homeowners" instead of a real range or named neighborhood)
- Overselling ("the best," "the most trusted," "unmatched quality")
- Contradictions with the knowledge base (wrong zip, wrong city, wrong pricing)
- Missing any one of: specific city/zip, specific dollar figure, specific material or method, CTA with phone number
- Reads like copy that could have been written about any contractor anywhere — no GHP-specific voice
